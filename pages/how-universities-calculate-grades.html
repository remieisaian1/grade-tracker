How UK university grades are usually calculated

UK universities usually calculate grades using a combination of assessment weights, module credits, year averages, and final-year weighting. The exact rules can change depending on the university, course, academic year, and award regulations.

This guide explains the common method, but it is not a replacement for your university’s official academic regulations.

1. What are university credits?

Credits show the size of a module. A larger-credit module has more impact on your year average than a smaller-credit module. For example, a 30-credit module usually counts twice as much as a 15-credit module.

Example:

Module A: 70% and 30 credits

Module B: 60% and 15 credits

Module A has more effect on the year average because it has more credits.

2. How module marks are calculated

A module often has multiple assessments, such as coursework, exams, presentations, reports, or projects. Each assessment normally has its own percentage weight.

Assessment Mark Weight
Coursework 70% 40%
Exam 60% 60%
Module mark = (70 × 0.4) + (60 × 0.6) = 64%

This module mark is then used in the credit-weighted year average.

3. How credit-weighted year averages work

The most common year-average calculation is a credit-weighted average. This means each module mark is multiplied by the number of credits for that module.

Year average = sum(module mark × module credits) ÷ total credits
Module Mark Credits Mark × Credits
Programming 70% 30 2100
Databases 60% 30 1800
Networking 50% 15 750
Web Dev 80% 15 1200
Software Eng 65% 30 1950
Total 120 7800
Year average = 7800 ÷ 120 = 65%

4. Credits are not percentages

A common mistake is thinking that getting 70% in a 20-credit module means you only receive 14 credits. That is not how it normally works.

Usually, if you pass a 20-credit module, you receive the full 20 credits. Your mark affects your average, but the module credit value stays the same.

5. How final degree grades are calculated

After each year average is calculated, universities usually apply a year weighting. Many UK universities do not count first year towards the final classification, or count it with little or no weight.

Final degree mark = (Year 2 average × Year 2 weight) + (Year 3 average × Year 3 weight)

Example:

Year 2 average = 62%

Year 3 average = 68%

Year 2 weight = 25%

Year 3 weight = 75%

Final = (62 × 0.25) + (68 × 0.75) = 66.5%

This would usually fall into the 2:1 classification range.

6. UK degree classification boundaries

Many UK undergraduate degrees use these broad classification boundaries:

Final average Classification
70%+ First Class / 1st
60–69% Upper Second / 2:1
50–59% Lower Second / 2:2
40–49% Third
Below 40% Fail

Some universities may also use borderline rules, rounding rules, compensation, or special classification rules.

The general maths is similar, but the final rules can be different between universities. This is why a calculator should allow custom year weighting and credit values.

8. Why your final result may differ

Your real university result may differ from a calculator estimate because universities can apply extra academic rules.

  • Different year weighting methods
  • Credit-weighted or best-credit calculations
  • Rounding or no-rounding rules
  • Borderline classification rules
  • Compensation rules for marginal fails
  • Resit or retake capped marks
  • Course-specific professional requirements
